## Section 1 — Executive summary

This benchmark measures all 17 Nexus agents against Wiz (continuous, multi-cloud, graph-prioritized CNAPP), the relevant point competitors, and Wazuh (as a clean-room source of extractable open-standard facts). The headline: Nexus is a credible, fast-moving challenger with genuine architectural leads — but defined v1.0 is not Wiz parity, and the reason is structural.

Read the parity column literally: **Narrower** means PRD v1.0 narrows a real gap but stays structurally short of Wiz — almost always because the v2.0 security-graph (blast-radius / toxic-combination / attack-path context) is the missing half, and that is platform substrate, not detector-maturity work.

### 1.2 The honest aggregate verdict

**Platform v1.0 — all 17 agents at their PRD/Level-3 internal scope — is materially narrower than Wiz, landing at ~75–80% of Wiz by weighted category, because Wiz is not a sum of detectors but detectors plus a security-graph plus two capability categories (AppSec, AI-SPM) that Nexus has not built — none of which is reachable by maturing the existing 17 agents.**

The ~20–25% residual decomposes into the v2.0 attack-graph layer (~14–19 pts, the dominant un-closed dimension in every detection agent's gap row, and currently gated by the SET LOCAL `$1` tenant-RLS bug at HEAD) and the two net-new agents (6 weighted pts, 0% today, with no owner agent in the 17). Category figures like "Compliance 100%" or "CDR 85%" are substrate-saturation or self-defined-category claims and must never be read as Wiz-feature parity.

### 1.3 Top 5 gaps that change strategy

1. **The v2.0 security-graph is the single largest residual** — attack-path / toxic-combination / blast-radius prioritization is the missing half of nearly every detection agent, and it is platform infrastructure currently blocked by the SET LOCAL `$1` tenant-RLS bug.
2. **Two whole categories have no owner agent** — AppSec/IaC/secrets-in-code/CI-CD (0.04 weight) and AI-SPM/SaaS posture (0.02 weight) sit at 0% and are net-new builds, not maturity work.
3. **D.1 Vulnerability is the biggest single lever after CSPM (0.13 weight) yet caps at ~55–65%** — container-images-only today, with no agentless snapshot scanning and no reachability/in-use prioritization even at v1.0.
4. **D.5 Multi-Cloud is strategically exposed** — Azure is a thin reformatter over a competitor's engine (Microsoft Defender), leaving the multi-cloud story dependent on a rival's output until a native Azure rule engine is built.
5. **D.2 Identity carries one of the largest absolute gaps** — AWS-IAM-only with a built-but-unused simulator path; multi-cloud effective-permissions and used-vs-granted analysis are unscoped.

### 1.4 Top 5 Wazuh patterns most worth extracting

All are open standards / facts re-derivable from primary catalogs — never Wazuh's GPL rule, decoder, or script bodies.

1. **MITRE ATT&CK technique → detection taxonomy** (MITRE-owned) — highest ROI, broadest reach; serves D.7 (10 → hundreds of techniques), D.4, D.8, D.12, and A.4 (an external coverage reward function attacking the drift #10 no-gradient problem). Run first.
2. **Compliance-framework control catalogs + cross-mappings** (PCI/HIPAA/NIST 800-53/TSC/GDPR/ISO) — takes Compliance from 1 framework to 6 clean-room and lets F.6/D.5/DSPM emit audit-grade tags.
3. **CVE feed-source roster + Syscollector inventory↔CTI correlation pattern** (NVD/OSV/CISA-KEV/MSU + 10+ per-vendor OVAL feeds) — cheapest path to D.1's "40+ OS" breadth and the architectural blueprint for the host/VM scanning D.1 lacks.
4. **CIS Benchmark control IDs** — turnkey hardening-check catalog for F.3 / D.5 / D.6 native-rule expansion at high breadth, low build cost.
5. **Active-Response stateful-revert playbook pattern** — the design blueprint for A.1's breadth direction and Supervisor's v0.2 retry/rollback contract.

### 1.5 Where Nexus honestly leads (differentiator axes)

These contribute **0 weighted Wiz points by design** — Wiz has no analog — so they neither raise nor should be hidden inside the Wiz-% number. They are the platform's real moat.

- **Edge** — the offline-capable, on-host operating posture distinct from Wiz's continuous-cloud model.
- **Cure / tiered remediation (A.1)** — a real closed loop _now_ (detect → patch+inverse → mandatory dry-run → execute → rollback-window → re-run → auto-revert) with a 4-stage earned-autonomy gate; Gartner cites Wiz's limited auto-remediation as a weakness, and A.1 is the one agent that already crossed offline→live.
- **Agentic loop (A.4 Meta-Harness)** — a customer-resident self-optimization loop (compose → eval-gate → auto-deploy, plus the default-OFF DSPy/GEPA optimizer) that no competitor ships; honest caveat: no measurable quality delta yet, which is why DSPy is default-OFF.
- **F.6 hash-chain audit** — genesis-rooted hash-chain + exit-status-2 tamper pin, a tamper-evident customer-verifiable audit-integrity capability the field does not center; honest caveat: the "saturated" claim holds only in single-tenant/jsonl mode until the tenant-RLS bug is fixed.

**One line for executives:** _Platform v1.0 is 100% of what we scoped and ~75–80% of Wiz by category weight; the final ~20–25% requires the v2.0 attack-graph and two net-new agents (AppSec, AI-SPM), not maturity work on the existing 17 — and we lead Wiz outright on five differentiator axes the Wiz-% number deliberately excludes._

## Section 2 — Wiz capability inventory

### 2.1 Positioning

Wiz is the category-defining agentless CNAPP: a unified, graph-driven cloud and AI security platform that connects to cloud-provider APIs in minutes to deliver posture, identity, data, vulnerability, and — via an optional eBPF sensor — runtime/detection coverage across multi-cloud. Acquired by Google for ~$32B. [sourced — wiz.io/platform]

The throughline across every capability area is the **Security Graph**: findings are not isolated alerts but nodes and edges in a continuously updated relationship map, which is what powers Wiz's signature contextual prioritization.

### 2.2 What Wiz does, by capability area

**CSPM** — Agentless, API-connected continuous configuration monitoring across IaaS/PaaS/SaaS, with sub-minute scan granularity and misconfiguration detection mapped to compliance frameworks. Posture findings feed into the Security Graph for contextual prioritization rather than surfacing as isolated alerts. [sourced — wiz.io/platform; wiz.io CSPM academy]

**KSPM** — Maps cluster architecture at the control-plane level, ingests the Kubernetes API-server audit log, and detects privileged/hostPID pods and misconfigurations. Control-plane-level cluster mapping is graphed alongside cloud resources. [sourced — solideinfo; loginsoft]

**CIEM** — Effective-access evaluation across identities and resources; flags over-privileged/misconfigured identities and exposes entitlement chains; supports Zero Standing Privilege (ZSP) and Just-in-Time (JIT) access. Effective permissions are computed on the Security Graph across cross-resource entitlement chains. [sourced — solideinfo; loginsoft]

**CWPP / runtime** — Agentless workload-risk scanning of VM/container/serverless images (packages, processes, services, secrets), plus an optional eBPF Runtime Sensor for live signals. The Sensor is lightweight, kernel-safe, and negligible-overhead, detecting miners, reverse shells, ransomware, rootkits, container escape, and lateral movement. [sourced — wiz.io Runtime Sensor; wiz.io CTO blog]

**Vulnerability management** — Agentless-first cross-cloud scanning covering 120,000+ vulnerabilities across 40+ operating systems; unified cloud/code/on-prem assessment, prioritization, and remediation. Catalog breadth combines with graph-based prioritization (exposure + sensitive-data context) to surface vulnerabilities that "truly impact." [sourced — wiz.io VM solution]

**DSPM** — Scans the actual content of buckets and databases (S3, RDS, Blob, BigQuery) for PII/PCI/PHI and alerts only at the intersection of sensitive data AND misconfiguration. Data-aware risk combines sensitivity and exposure on the graph. [sourced — loginsoft]

**CDR (Wiz Defend)** — Cloud-first detection that starts from Security Graph context, analyzes cloud/Kubernetes events, then layers eBPF runtime signals, correlating attacker movement across layers. Ordering is graph → cloud/K8s logs → runtime, with blast-radius computed via the graph. [sourced — wiz.io Wiz Defend; wiz.io CDR academy]

**Network exposure** — Identifies internet-facing assets and models network exposure as graph attributes/edges feeding attack-path analysis. Deep packet/segmentation analysis is not detailed. [unknown depth] [sourced as omission — wiz.io evaluate FAQ]

**Compliance** — Continuous compliance status against multiple frameworks, with posture/compliance dashboards; framework mapping is built on CSPM findings. [sourced — wiz.io/platform; solideinfo]

**Remediation (Wiz Actions / Code)** — Guided/one-click remediation, GenAI step-by-step fix guidance, code-level fixes via PRs, and cloud-to-code hardening. Code-to-cloud PR remediation is detection-first with guided response rather than autonomous execution (see 2.4). [sourced — wiz.io/platform; Gartner Peer Insights]

**Threat Intel** — The Wiz Threat Center surfaces workload exposure to emerging/in-the-wild threats, checked against NVD + CISA KEV + curated feeds, overlaying threat-in-the-wild exposure on the inventory graph. [sourced — wiz.io VM academy]

**AI-SPM** — Discovers Shadow AI (unmanaged models, training data, AI API endpoints), builds an AI-BOM, extends DSPM to training data, and performs graph-based attack-path analysis to AI assets. The Wiz Sensor stops prompt injection, rogue agents, and malicious AI behavior at runtime. [sourced — wiz.io AI-SPM; wiz.io/platform]

**SaaS posture (SSPM)** — Not a native first-party strength; SaaS config posture is addressed largely via partner integration (e.g., AppOmni). Native SSPM is a noted gap. [sourced — appomni blog; wiz.io evaluate FAQ]

### 2.3 Wiz signature features

1. **Security Graph** — A continuously updated, queryable relationship map of every resource, identity, workload, data store, network connection, vulnerability, and finding; the foundational data layer all modules share. [sourced]
2. **Toxic-combination & attack-path analysis** — Chains individually low/medium findings (e.g., vuln + public exposure + admin IAM role) into critical, attacker-exploitable risk paths. [sourced]
3. **Agentless snapshot scanning** — API-connected scanning of VM/container/serverless images for packages, processes, and secrets without per-host agents; deploys in minutes. [sourced]
4. **Effective-permissions (CIEM on the graph)** — Computes true effective access across cross-resource entitlement chains, surfacing over-privilege that static policy review misses. [sourced — solideinfo; loginsoft]
5. **Code-to-cloud** — Zero-config mapping linking running cloud resources back to originating code and developers; Wiz Code shows whether a vulnerable code path is actually deployed, exposed, and data-connected. [sourced]
6. **DSPM** — Content-level scanning of data stores that alerts only where sensitivity and exposure intersect. [sourced — loginsoft]
7. **AI-SPM** — AI-BOM, DSPM-for-training-data, AI attack-path analysis, plus runtime AI threat detection via the Wiz Sensor. [sourced — wiz.io AI-SPM]
8. **Wiz (Runtime) Sensor** — Lightweight, kernel-safe eBPF sensor providing live runtime detection (miners, reverse shells, ransomware, rootkits, container escape, lateral movement) and runtime AI threat prevention. [sourced — wiz.io Runtime Sensor; wiz.io CTO blog]

### 2.4 What Wiz does NOT do — Nexus differentiation openings

- **No edge / OT; thin on-prem.** Coverage is cloud-API-bound; on-prem is limited to private cloud via VMware vSphere, and OT/edge is entirely absent. _(Differentiation opening for Nexus.)_ [sourced — wiz.io evaluate FAQ]
- **Detection-first, limited auto-remediation.** Emphasis on one-click/guided/PR remediation; autonomous remediation execution is not the model, and reviewers cite "limited runtime protection vs. full CWPP/EDR." _(Cure / auto-remediation depth opening.)_ [sourced — Gartner Peer Insights]
- **Cloud-API-bound visibility.** Agentless is "primary," implying gaps wherever APIs can't reach; full runtime requires the optional eBPF sensor, which is not universal. _(Cloud-API-bound limitation.)_ [sourced — wiz.io evaluate FAQ]
- **Enterprise pricing / consumption costs.** Subscription is priced by workloads/resources/assets plus consumption-based features; reviewers cite high licensing cost at large cloud scale. _(Pricing opening.)_ [sourced — Gartner Peer Insights; G2]
- **Complexity & tuning burden.** Advanced features are hard to navigate and require tuning to reduce noise/false positives; repo-to-project mapping is awkward. [sourced — Gartner Peer Insights]
- **Native SSPM gap.** SaaS posture leans on partner integrations (AppOmni) rather than deep first-party SSPM. [sourced — AppOmni; wiz.io evaluate FAQ]
- **Network analysis depth [unknown].** Exposure is modeled, but deep traffic/segmentation analysis is not documented. [sourced as omission — wiz.io evaluate FAQ]

## Section 3 — Other competitors

This section widens the benchmark beyond Wiz, profiling six additional CNAPP platforms — Orca Security, Lacework (Fortinet FortiCNAPP), Sysdig Secure, Palo Alto Prisma Cloud, CrowdStrike Falcon Cloud Security, and Microsoft Defender for Cloud — across each capability area. Tags ([sourced], [estimate], [unknown], [training-knowledge]) and source URLs are preserved from the research.

### 3.0 One-line positioning

- **Orca Security** — Agentless-first CNAPP built on patented SideScanning (reads workload block storage; "no packets, no code") with a Unified Data Model graph for attack-path prioritization across AWS/Azure/GCP/OCI/Alibaba. [sourced]
- **Lacework (Fortinet FortiCNAPP)** — Behavioral-analytics-first CNAPP centered on the patented Polygraph engine (unsupervised-ML baselines, alert-on-deviation); acquired by Fortinet (Aug 2024), now folded into the Security Fabric. [sourced]

- **Palo Alto Prisma Cloud** — Broadest, most feature-complete code-to-cloud CNAPP, assembled from acquisitions (RedLock, Twistlock, Bridgecrew, Cider), now transitioning to "Cortex Cloud." [sourced]
- **CrowdStrike Falcon Cloud Security** — Endpoint-first, CWPP-heavy CNAPP extending Falcon EDR sensor DNA and adversary intelligence into cloud workloads and the control plane. [sourced]
- **Microsoft Defender for Cloud** — Azure-native "good-enough default": free foundational CSPM + Secure Score on every subscription, with paid Defender CSPM/CWPP plans layered on. [sourced]

#### B) Honest narrative

**Is PRD v1.0 = Wiz parity, or narrower? Neither — it is orthogonal.** There is no Wiz Supervisor to be at parity _with_. Wiz, Orca, Prisma, CrowdStrike, Sysdig and Defender are all **monolithic** platforms: a single detection/graph engine with internal microservices. None expose a "supervisor agent" as a capability, because none are built as a fleet of autonomous, charter-governed specialist agents. The Supervisor exists _because_ Nexus chose multi-agent architecture — it is the connective tissue that the PRD's headline differentiator ("multi-agent autonomous SOC, 14–18 specialized agents under runtime charter") structurally requires. So Supervisor is not a coverage-gap line item; it is **infrastructure for a differentiator**. The honest framing: PRD v1.0's Supervisor target is _narrower than the PRD's own autonomy rhetoric_ (v0.1 is deliberately a no-LLM rule-router with one-attempt dispatch and 10/17 routability — the verification record is explicit that the narrowness was chosen to avoid pre-committing the v0.2 design), but it is exactly **on-spec for what the platform needs at this stage** and has no external parity bar to clear.

**Where v0.1 honestly sits on the differentiator axis.** The autonomous-SOC claim has three load-bearing parts: (1) specialized agents — 17 shipped; (2) a charter enforcing execution contracts and self-evolution — present (F.1 budgets, F.6 audit, the A.4 meta-harness); and (3) **autonomous coordination** — this is Supervisor, and it is the **least mature of the three**. Today it does _routing and dispatch_, not _planning_. It fans out tasks the caller has **pre-declared** as independent (the README is careful: "NOT multi-agent planning"); it routes via a hand-curated YAML table, not inference; it covers only the 10 Path-A specialists, leaving the 6 Path-B agents (data*security, threat_intel, compliance, synthesis, curiosity, meta_harness) reachable only by their own CLIs/event triggers; it makes one attempt with no retry; and it is single-tenant and stateless per tick. So the \_autonomous* in "autonomous SOC" is, at v0.1, a 60-second heartbeat loop draining a file-backed queue through static rules — real, deterministic, audited, but a long way from a planning orchestrator. **The single most credible thing in v0.1 is the safety posture, not the autonomy:** the Task 8 `_FORBIDDEN_SUBSCRIPTIONS["supervisor"]` fence (the only SAFETY-CRITICAL PR in the cycle) prevents Supervisor from ever reading speculation off `claims.>` and laundering a Curiosity hypothesis into an A.1 auto-remediation. That is genuinely ahead of the market's thinking — no competitor has had to solve "stop the orchestrator from turning a guess into a destructive action" because none have autonomous-remediation-routing orchestrators.

**Competitor features on NO roadmap (relevant to this agent).** Because there's no orchestration competitor, the relevant absence is the inverse: the _AI-assistant_ paradigm everyone else ships (Prisma AgentiX, CrowdStrike Charlotte AI, Sysdig Sage, Defender's Copilot tie-in) is a **conversational analyst copilot** — multistep reasoning over search/triage/response that a human drives. Nexus has **no conversational-copilot surface on the Supervisor roadmap at all**; the v0.2 plan is LLM-_assisted routing_ and multi-agent _planning_, which is a different (more autonomous, less human-in-the-loop) bet. That is a deliberate divergence, not an oversight, but it is worth flagging honestly: if buyers expect a Sage/Charlotte-style chat copilot, the Supervisor as specced does not provide one — D.7 Investigation + D.13 Synthesis carry the human-facing reasoning, and the Supervisor stays a backend coordinator. Conversely, **multi-tenant orchestration** is on the roadmap but **blocked** by the platform-wide `SET LOCAL $1` tenant-RLS substrate bug — the Supervisor explicitly defers multi-tenant production to that fix, so this differentiator cannot be demonstrated at scale until that blocker clears.

**Wazuh patterns specifically relevant to this agent.** Wazuh is a telemetry/HIDS platform, not an orchestrator, so direct reuse is thin — but two structural patterns are clean-room-extractable and relevant. (1) **Rule-ID-keyed active response**: Wazuh triggers reversible (auto-revert-on-timeout) vs irreversible countermeasures keyed to a rule ID/level/group. The _pattern_ (a stateful action keyed to a detection identity, with timeout-bounded reversibility) is a fact, not GPL code, and maps directly onto how Supervisor should sequence dispatch→A.1-remediation→rollback — Supervisor's time-boxed one-attempt model could borrow Wazuh's stateful/stateless distinction for the v0.2 retry/rollback contract. (2) **Manager-driven single-threaded-per-agent coordination**: Wazuh's manager serializes per-agent work; Supervisor's `fcntl.flock` per-customer single-threading is the same concurrency-safety instinct, validating the v0.1 choice. Neither requires copying Wazuh's GPL implementation — both are abstract patterns. There is no Wazuh equivalent to LLM-assisted routing or multi-agent planning, so the v0.2+ surface is greenfield.

## Section 7 — Honest reframing of Platform v1.0

### 7.1 The core question

Does "Platform v1.0 = all 17 agents at their PRD/Level-3 internal scope" equal Wiz parity?

**No. It is materially narrower — and the gap is structural, not a matter of detector tuning.** The internal accounting and the 17 per-agent analyses converge on a single, defensible answer: a fully-executed Platform v1.0 lands at roughly **75–80% of Wiz on the weighted-category math**, and the residual ~20–25 points are not reachable by maturing the existing 17 agents at all. They require building things that are out of the v1.0 definition by construction.

### 7.2 The evidence that v1.0 ≠ parity

Three independent lines of evidence, all from measured/cited internal sources, establish the cap.

**(a) The detection-maturity ceiling is ~75–80%, and the curve flattens there.** Per `agent-detection-maturity-v0-1-to-v0-3-2026-06-07.md`, the platform forecast is:

| Phase          | Weighted Wiz % | What unlocks it                                                                     |
| -------------- | -------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| v0.1 (now)     | ~56–60%        | 17 agents shipped, offline + AWS-focused                                            |

| Wiz parity     | 100%           | **requires 2 net-new agents + the v2.0 security-graph**                             |

The verbatim verdict from that document: detection maturity to v0.3 is _"necessary but not sufficient"_ for parity; the last ~20–25% _"requires (a) building two entirely new agent categories and (b) standing up the v2.0 attack-path graph — neither are detection-agent maturity improvements."_ Level-3 maturity on the existing 17 agents is exactly this detection-maturity arc. It tops out below parity.

**(b) The weighted accounting shows where the un-closeable points live.** Two whole categories sit at 0% with no agent assigned in the 17:

| Category                               | Weight | v1.0 coverage | Why it stays 0 in v1.0                    |
| -------------------------------------- | ------ | ------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| AppSec / IaC / secrets-in-code / CI-CD | 0.04   | **0%**        | No agent exists in the 17 — net-new build |
| AI-SPM / SaaS Posture                  | 0.02   | **0%**        | No agent exists in the 17 — net-new build |

That is **6 weighted points (0.06) that no amount of Level-3 maturity on the 17 agents can recover**, because the capability has no owner agent. The remaining gap is concentrated in the per-agent analyses, where the recurring phrase is identical across F.3, D.1, D.2, D.3, D.4, DSPM, D.6, D.7: each agent's _own_ honest single-agent Wiz coverage at PRD v1.0 lands at **~45–65%**, and in every case the residual is attributed to **the missing v2.0 security-graph** (toxic-combination / attack-path / blast-radius prioritization), not to detector breadth.

**(c) The "100% category" figures are substrate-saturation claims, not parity claims.** The internal table shows Compliance at 100% and CDR at 85% — but the per-agent reads correct this directly. Compliance "100%" means _audit-substrate saturation_ (1 paraphrased framework, 15 of 44 controls wired, offline, AWS-only, sibling-bounded); benchmarked against Wiz's actual continuous multi-framework graph-contextualized compliance, the honest single-agent number is **~10–15% at v0.1, ~40–55% at PRD v1.0**. The category percentages are real on their own axis but must never be read as Wiz-feature parity.

### 7.3 The structural gap, named precisely

The detector-maturity finding is the load-bearing one: **detector maturity caps platform coverage at ~75–80% of Wiz.** The cap exists because Wiz is not a sum of detectors — it is detectors _plus a security graph_ that turns isolated findings into prioritized attack paths, _plus two capability categories Nexus has not built_. Concretely, the residual ~20–25% decomposes into:

1. **The v2.0 attack-graph / security-graph layer (~14–19 pts of residual).** Wiz's signature move — "a public bucket + an admin role + a vuln = one critical attack path, not three medium alerts" — appears in every detection agent's gap row as the dominant un-closed dimension. It is platform infrastructure, not an agent feature, and it is currently **gated by the SET LOCAL `$1` tenant-RLS bug** present at HEAD (`charter/memory/service.py:96`), which breaks multi-tenant isolation on real Postgres and blocks the SemanticStore-as-graph strategy.

2. **The 2 net-new agents (6 pts, 0.06 weight).** AppSec/IaC/secrets and AI-SPM/SaaS — zero agents today, each ~3–4 week builds, outside the 17.

These two items, by definition, are _not_ "the 17 agents at Level-3." That is precisely why v1.0 ≠ parity.

### 7.4 What TRUE 100% Wiz parity would require

Parity is the union of four distinct workstreams, only the first of which is "mature the 17 agents":

| Requirement                     | What it is                                                                                                                                                                                                                  | In v1.0 (17 agents @ L3)?                                       |
| ------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **(1) Detection maturity**      | The 17 existing agents matured offline→live, multi-cloud, full rule breadth (F.3 live boto3, D.5 native Azure/GCP rules, D.1 host/VM/serverless + reachability, D.2 multi-cloud effective-perms, DSPM ML+multi-store, etc.) | **Yes** — this _is_ v1.0, and it reaches ~75–80%                |

### 7.5 Recommended honest framing language for maturity claims

The repeated error to eliminate is averaging a substrate-saturation percentage or a self-defined-category percentage into an implied Wiz-parity claim. The fix is a fixed template that always names the denominator.

**Replace this:**

- ❌ "Platform v1.0 = 100% mature."
- ❌ "Compliance is at 100%." / "CDR is at 85%."
- ❌ "We're at ~56–60% of Wiz" (stated without the cap).

**With this:**

- ✅ **"Platform v1.0 = 100% of our defined v1.0 scope, which is ~75–80% of Wiz by weighted category. The remaining ~20–25% is not detector work — it is the v2.0 security-graph plus two net-new agents (AppSec, AI-SPM)."**
- ✅ For any category figure, always tag the axis: **"Compliance: 100% of audit-substrate saturation (≈10–15% of Wiz's continuous multi-framework graph-contextualized compliance today)."** / **"CDR: 85% of our forensic-correlation category (≈30–40% of Wiz Defend, which adds graph blast-radius + runtime correlation)."**
- ✅ For per-agent claims, use two numbers and never average them: **"single-agent Wiz coverage [estimate]"** and, where relevant, **"differentiator-axis maturity [estimate]"**, tagged.

**Three drop-in framing rules:**

1. **Always state the denominator.** Every "% mature" or "% complete" must read "% of _[named scope]_." "100% of defined v1.0 scope" and "~78% of Wiz-in-category" are both true and must travel together.

2. **Separate coverage claims from differentiator claims; never blend them.** The differentiator agents — **A.4 Meta-Harness, #0 Supervisor, D.12 Curiosity, F.6 Audit-integrity, A.1 autonomous-cure** — contribute **0 weighted Wiz points by design** (Wiz has no analog), so they neither raise nor should be hidden inside the Wiz-% number. State them on their own axis: "0% of Wiz (no analog); a category we lead." Conversely, head-to-head agents (CSPM, Vuln, CIEM, CWPP, DSPM) are "catch-up, plainly behind, large absolute gap" and should be stated as such.

3. **Surface the gate, not just the number.** Any parity timeline must name that the v2.0 graph — the single largest residual — is blocked at HEAD by the SET LOCAL `$1` tenant-RLS bug. The honest claim is: _"v1.0 caps at ~75–80% of Wiz; closing further is gated on a platform substrate (the security-graph) that is itself currently blocked by a known multi-tenant isolation bug — so the remaining 20–25% is a multi-quarter platform program, not a detector-tuning sprint."_

**One-line summary for executive use:** _"Platform v1.0 is 100% of what we scoped and ~75–80% of Wiz by category weight; the final ~20–25% requires the v2.0 attack-graph and two net-new agents (AppSec, AI-SPM), not maturity work on the existing 17 — and we lead Wiz outright on five differentiator axes that the Wiz-% number deliberately excludes."_
